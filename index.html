Statement of Artist

감각, 관계, 그리고 실재의 조건

I have long explored the boundaries between reality and fiction. This pursuit wasn't driven merely by conceptual curiosity. Though I was always the same person, others perceived me differently depending on my geographical context: in Italy, I was seen as Korean; in Korea, I was seen as Italian. My identity was never something fixed within me; it was instead a narrative continuously rewritten through others' eyes and environmental contexts. Through this experience, I came to realize that reality is not a single fixed truth, but rather a fluid state continuously formed by relationships and storytelling.

My work began with fake documentaries and expanded into XR and virtual spaces. In producing fake documentaries, I tested the persuasive power embedded in their formal elements - the calm tone, specific editing rhythms, and visual compositions that resemble archival recordings - which easily led viewers to accept fictional narratives as truth. In XR, I more directly challenged the boundaries of belief. In exhibitions, viewers interact with virtual objects that clearly do not exist, experiencing immediate sensory confusion at the boundary between real and virtual. This moment prompts viewers to reconsider their own beliefs and how these beliefs are formed.

My primary interest isn't in answering "What is true?" but rather in exploring why and how we come to believe something is true. Images flood our daily lives, yet we rarely question the basis of our belief in their authenticity. I am cautious of our indifference toward the mechanisms by which beliefs are formed - beliefs that emerge easily and spread rapidly, often solidifying into unquestioned certainty. My work aims to reveal these mechanisms, encouraging viewers to consciously reflect on what they believe and why.

This motivation led me to generative AI. Initially, I used AI to produce a vast number of technically impressive yet random images, but found it challenging to feel any emotional connection or meaningful dialogue with my creations. By chance, I incorporated the image of my spouse, someone with whom I share an authentic emotional bond, into my work. For the first time, I experienced a profound personal connection with the resulting piece. Since then, I have consistently used this approach.

To the audience, the face in my work may appear simply as an unfamiliar person, but to me, it embodies traces of my closest emotional connection. This discrepancy creates distinct viewing experiences for myself and my audience. Though I never explicitly disclose this personal dimension within the work, I have often witnessed viewers significantly narrowing their emotional distance upon learning this background later on. In these moments, the artwork transitions from an object of interpretation to an experience rooted in relationships and emotions.

In my works, I emphasize human figures and the gaze. The figures consistently look directly at the audience. Humans instinctively respond more strongly to living beings than objects, more so to humans among all beings, and most intensely to the eyes among all human features. This gaze transforms the audience from active observers to passive recipients of being watched. This shift is communicated not through explanation but through an immediate, bodily experience. I believe this visceral response forms the foundation of our judgments about authenticity.

I have repeatedly observed audiences lingering even in front of nearly motionless screens. During an outdoor LED exhibition, preschool children showed little interest in visually elaborate works nearby but would pull their parents toward my piece, pointing at it intently. They responded not to explicit narrative or explanation, but to the primal stimulus of contrast and direct gaze - precisely the intended effect of my work.

Finally, I recall Diego Velazquez's Las Meninas. The painting captures a scene from the perspective of King Philip IV, depicting his daughter, her attendants, and the painter himself - a scene undeniably real for the king. Similarly, my sense of authenticity in my work arises from the personal memory of the figure being my spouse. Ultimately, truth changes according to the viewer's position and memories.

This is the question I continuously pose through my practice:
What are we looking at, and on what grounds do we believe it to be real?
